# UX Governance Add-on

## Purpose

This document provides governance extensions for the UX and User-Flow phase of AI product development. It ensures that user interfaces involving AI agents -- customer-facing chatbots, autonomous decision-makers, and recommendation engines -- are designed to preserve user agency, enable informed consent, and prevent manipulative patterns. In regulated FinTech, a user's ability to understand, control, and override AI-driven interactions is not optional -- it is a regulatory requirement.

This add-on is designed to be used alongside an external AI Product Development Toolkit. It injects governance questions and checkpoints into UX reviews, design critiques, and usability testing so that fairness and transparency are embedded at the interaction layer.

**This document does not replace the AI Product Dev Toolkit's UX process. It extends it with governance-specific content.**

## When to Use

- When designing or reviewing UX flows that involve AI-powered features (chatbots, recommendations, automated decisions)
- When conducting UX design reviews or design critiques for AI-enabled products
- When running usability tests that involve AI interactions
- When onboarding UX designers who are new to AI governance requirements in regulated financial services
- As a reference during accessibility audits of AI-powered interfaces

**Trigger:** A UX designer creates or updates a user flow, wireframe, or prototype for a feature that involves AI/ML/LLM interaction.

## Who Is Responsible

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **UX Designer** | **Responsible** -- applies governance checklist during design, ensures all UX governance questions are addressed |
| **Product Manager** | **Accountable** -- ensures UX governance review is completed before design sign-off |
| **Accessibility Lead** | **Consulted** -- reviews accessibility of AI interactions for users with disabilities |
| **Ethics Lead** | **Consulted** -- reviews informed consent flows and dark pattern assessment |
| **Compliance Officer (2nd Line)** | **Informed** -- receives evidence of UX governance review for limited/high-risk systems |

## Regulatory Basis

- **EU AI Act Article 50(1)** -- Persons interacting with AI must be informed they are interacting with AI, unless obvious from context
- **EU AI Act Article 14** -- High-risk AI systems must enable effective human oversight, including the ability to understand, interpret, and intervene
- **EU AI Act Article 86** -- Right to explanation for decisions by high-risk AI systems affecting individuals
- **GDPR Article 13/14** -- Right to information about automated decision-making, including meaningful information about the logic involved
- **GDPR Article 22** -- Right not to be subject to solely automated decision-making with legal or similarly significant effects
- **DORA Article 11** -- ICT-related incident communication to clients
- **SAFEST T-06** -- User disclosure of AI involvement
- **SAFEST T-07** -- Explainability of AI decisions to affected persons
- **SAFEST E-09** -- Avoiding manipulative design that exploits user vulnerabilities
- **Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 AA** -- Accessibility of digital interfaces (FinTech regulatory expectation via PSD2 accessibility requirements)

## Section 1: AI Transparency in User Interfaces

Users must know when they are interacting with an AI system, what role the AI plays in their experience, and where the boundary between AI and human decision-making lies.

### 1.1 AI Disclosure Design Checklist

| # | Requirement | Status | Notes |
|---|-------------|--------|-------|
| 1 | The interface clearly discloses when the user is interacting with an AI agent (chatbot, virtual assistant, automated advisor) | [ ] | EU AI Act Art. 50(1) |
| 2 | AI disclosure is visible without user action -- not hidden in tooltips, footer text, or settings | [ ] | |
| 3 | When AI generates or influences content displayed to the user (recommendations, risk scores, summaries), the AI origin is indicated | [ ] | |
| 4 | The disclosure language is plain and jargon-free ("This response was generated by AI" not "Powered by ML model v3.2") | [ ] | |
| 5 | For multi-channel interactions, AI disclosure is consistent across web, mobile, and voice interfaces | [ ] | |
| 6 | When a conversation is handed off from AI to a human agent (or vice versa), the transition is explicitly communicated | [ ] | |

### 1.2 Explanation Design

When an AI system makes or influences a decision that affects a user, the interface must provide an explanation.

| Decision Type | Explanation Requirement | Example |
|--------------|------------------------|---------|
| **Adverse financial decision** (loan denial, account restriction, transaction decline) | Proactive explanation: displayed automatically without user request | "Your application was not approved because your debt-to-income ratio exceeds our threshold. You can appeal this decision." |
| **Risk assessment** (fraud score, credit score, AML flag) | On-request explanation: available via a clear "Why?" affordance | "Why was this transaction flagged?" button with contextual explanation |
| **Recommendation** (product suggestion, investment advice, spending insight) | Contextual explanation: brief rationale alongside the recommendation | "Suggested because you have recurring payments in this category" |
| **Content filtering** (blocked message, restricted action) | Notification with reason: user knows why their action was blocked | "This transfer was paused for additional verification because it exceeds your usual pattern" |

### 1.3 Mental Model Alignment

The interface must help users form an accurate mental model of what the AI can and cannot do.

| # | Question | Your Answer |
|---|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Does the interface set accurate expectations about the AI's capabilities? (No implied omniscience) | |
| 2 | Does the interface communicate the AI's limitations? (What it cannot do, where it may be wrong) | |
| 3 | Are confidence indicators displayed where appropriate? (e.g., "High confidence" vs. "I'm less certain about this") | |
| 4 | Does the interface avoid anthropomorphization that could lead users to over-trust the AI? (No "I understand your feelings" from a chatbot) | |
| 5 | If the AI makes a mistake, does the interface make it easy for the user to recognize and correct the error? | |

> *FinTech example:* A lending chatbot that says "I can help you understand your options" is acceptable. A chatbot that says "I will find you the perfect loan" creates a misleading mental model and may violate fair lending disclosure requirements.

## Section 2: User Control and Agency

Users must have meaningful control over AI-driven interactions. Control must be granular -- not just a binary on/off switch.

### 2.1 Control Granularity Framework

| Control Level | Description | When Required | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| **Full opt-out** | User can disable AI entirely and use a non-AI alternative | All risk tiers | "Use classic (non-AI) transaction search" |
| **Feature-level control** | User can enable/disable specific AI features independently | Limited and High-risk | Toggle AI categorization on/off while keeping AI fraud alerts on |
| **Interaction-level control** | User can override or reject individual AI decisions/suggestions | High-risk | "This categorization is wrong" with manual correction |
| **Data-level control** | User can control what data the AI uses about them | Where consent is the lawful basis | "Don't use my location data for spending insights" |
| **Communication-level control** | User can choose how the AI communicates with them | All risk tiers | "Show me detailed explanations" vs. "Show me summaries" |

### 2.2 Override and Appeal Mechanisms

| # | Requirement | Status | Notes |
|---|-------------|--------|-------|
| 1 | Every AI-generated decision that affects the user has a visible override or dispute mechanism | [ ] | EU AI Act Art. 14(4)(d) |
| 2 | The override/dispute mechanism is no more than 2 clicks/taps from the decision point | [ ] | |
| 3 | Override actions are acknowledged immediately (not queued silently) | [ ] | |
| 4 | For consequential decisions (credit, account actions), a human review path is available | [ ] | GDPR Art. 22 |
| 5 | The appeal process is documented and communicated to the user before the decision is made | [ ] | |
| 6 | Override and appeal rates are tracked as governance metrics | [ ] | |

### 2.3 Consent and Preference Management

| # | Question | Your Answer |
|---|----------|-------------|
| 1 | Is consent for AI processing collected separately from general terms and conditions? | |
| 2 | Can the user withdraw consent for AI processing without losing access to the core service? | |
| 3 | Are AI preferences easily accessible (not buried in settings hierarchies)? | |
| 4 | Are preference changes applied immediately, not "at next login" or "within 24 hours"? | |
| 5 | Is the consent language specific about what the AI does, what data it uses, and what decisions it influences? | |

> *FinTech example:* A banking app that requires users to accept AI-powered fraud detection to use the app at all may be justified (legitimate interest under GDPR). But AI-powered spending insights that require separate consent should allow users to opt out without losing access to core banking functionality.

## Section 3: Accessibility of AI Interactions

AI-powered interfaces must be accessible to users with disabilities. This is both a regulatory obligation and a fairness requirement -- if AI features are inaccessible, they create a two-tier service.

### 3.1 Accessibility Checklist for AI Features

| # | Requirement | WCAG Ref | Status | Notes |
|---|-------------|----------|--------|-------|
| 1 | AI-generated text content meets WCAG readability guidelines (plain language, appropriate reading level) | 3.1.5 | [ ] | |
| 2 | AI chatbot interfaces are fully keyboard navigable | 2.1.1 | [ ] | |
| 3 | AI-generated content is compatible with screen readers (proper ARIA labels, semantic HTML) | 4.1.2 | [ ] | |
| 4 | AI-generated images, charts, or visualizations have text alternatives | 1.1.1 | [ ] | |
| 5 | AI response timing accommodates users who need more time (no auto-dismissing AI suggestions) | 2.2.1 | [ ] | |
| 6 | AI disclosure and explanation text meets minimum contrast ratios | 1.4.3 | [ ] | |
| 7 | Voice-based AI interactions provide text alternatives | 1.2.1 | [ ] | |
| 8 | Error messages from AI systems are descriptive and suggest corrective actions | 3.3.1, 3.3.3 | [ ] | |
| 9 | AI-powered form auto-completion can be disabled by the user | 1.3.5 | [ ] | |
| 10 | AI interaction patterns do not rely solely on color to convey meaning (e.g., red/green for approval/denial) | 1.4.1 | [ ] | |

### 3.2 Cognitive Accessibility for AI Interactions

| # | Consideration | Status | Notes |
|---|---------------|--------|-------|
| 1 | AI explanations are layered: simple summary first, details on demand | [ ] | |
| 2 | AI does not present too many options simultaneously (cognitive overload) | [ ] | |
| 3 | AI interactions follow predictable patterns -- no surprise behaviors | [ ] | |
| 4 | Technical AI terminology is replaced with plain language equivalents | [ ] | |
| 5 | Users can request the AI to rephrase or simplify its response | [ ] | |

> *FinTech example:* A credit decision explanation that says "Your application score of 0.73 fell below the 0.80 threshold on the logistic regression model" is inaccessible. Instead: "Your application was not approved. The main reason was that your monthly debt payments are high compared to your income. Here is what you can do next."

## Section 4: Dark Pattern Prevention

AI-powered interfaces create new categories of manipulative design that traditional dark pattern frameworks do not cover. This section extends dark pattern prevention to AI-specific risks.

### 4.1 AI-Specific Dark Pattern Checklist

| # | Dark Pattern | Description | Check | Status |
|---|-------------|-------------|-------|--------|
| 1 | **Artificial urgency via AI** | AI creates false urgency ("This offer expires soon" based on no real deadline) | Does the AI ever create urgency that is not based on real constraints? | [ ] |
| 2 | **Personalized manipulation** | AI uses personal data to craft individually targeted persuasion | Does the AI tailor its language to exploit known user vulnerabilities? | [ ] |
| 3 | **Forced AI interaction** | Users cannot complete essential tasks without engaging with AI | Can users accomplish core tasks through a non-AI path? | [ ] |
| 4 | **Asymmetric friction** | Easy to accept AI recommendation, hard to reject or override it | Is the effort to accept and reject AI suggestions equivalent? | [ ] |
| 5 | **Confidence theater** | AI presents uncertain outputs with false confidence | Does the AI communicate uncertainty honestly? | [ ] |
| 6 | **Anchoring bias exploitation** | AI presents a high initial value to anchor user expectations | Does the AI use anchoring techniques in pricing, scoring, or recommendations? | [ ] |
| 7 | **Social proof manipulation** | AI fabricates or selectively presents social proof ("Other users like you chose X") | Are social proof claims verifiable and representative? | [ ] |
| 8 | **Nagging and persistence** | AI repeatedly suggests actions the user has declined | Does the AI respect user refusals? How many times can it re-suggest? | [ ] |
| 9 | **Hidden AI influence** | AI subtly influences decisions without user awareness (e.g., ordering of options) | Are users aware of all ways the AI influences what they see? | [ ] |
| 10 | **Emotional manipulation** | AI uses emotional language to influence financial decisions | Does the AI use emotional appeals in contexts involving money? | [ ] |

### 4.2 FinTech-Specific Dark Pattern Risks

| Scenario | Risk | Governance Requirement |
|----------|------|----------------------|
| AI-powered upselling in banking app | AI uses spending data to time product offers when user is most susceptible | Upsell recommendations must be clearly labeled as promotional; user can disable |
| Chatbot handling complaints | AI uses sentiment analysis to de-escalate without resolving the issue | Complaint chatbots must offer human escalation within 2 interactions |
| Automated investment advice | AI presents risk in a way that encourages higher-risk products (which generate more fees) | Risk presentation must be balanced; fees must be equally prominent |
| Savings goal recommendations | AI sets unrealistic savings goals to increase engagement | Goals must be based on user's actual financial situation |
| Loan pre-approval notifications | AI sends pre-approval alerts to users who may not be able to afford the loan | Pre-approval messaging must include affordability warnings |

## Section 5: Governance Questions for UX Reviews

Inject these questions into every UX design review or design critique where AI features are involved. Each question maps to a governance principle and a regulatory requirement.

### 5.1 Pre-Design Review Checklist

Complete before beginning detailed UX design for any AI-powered feature.

| # | Question | Governance Principle | Regulatory Ref | Answer |
|---|----------|---------------------|----------------|--------|
| 1 | Who is the most vulnerable user who will encounter this AI interaction? What is their experience? | Fairness / Inclusion | SAFEST E-09 | |
| 2 | If the AI is wrong, how will the user know? How will they correct it? | User Agency | EU AI Act Art. 14 | |
| 3 | Can the user complete this task without AI involvement if they choose to? | User Control | GDPR Art. 22 | |
| 4 | What does the user need to understand about the AI to make informed decisions? | Transparency | EU AI Act Art. 50 | |
| 5 | Is the AI interaction accessible to users with visual, motor, cognitive, or hearing disabilities? | Accessibility | WCAG 2.1 AA | |
| 6 | Does any element of this design create asymmetric friction (easier to accept AI than to reject it)? | Dark Pattern Prevention | SAFEST E-09 | |
| 7 | How will we test this interaction with real users from diverse backgrounds? | Fairness Validation | SAFEST F-03 | |

### 5.2 Design Critique Governance Questions

Ask during design critique sessions.

| # | Question | What to Look For |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | "Show me the AI disclosure in this flow." | Is it visible, clear, and early enough? |
| 2 | "Walk me through what happens when the AI is wrong." | Is error recovery smooth and user-controlled? |
| 3 | "Show me the non-AI alternative path." | Does it exist? Is it equally accessible? |
| 4 | "How does a screen reader user experience this AI interaction?" | Full keyboard navigation, proper ARIA, text alternatives |
| 5 | "Show me where the user can override the AI." | Is override visible, not hidden, and within 2 clicks? |
| 6 | "What data is the AI using, and does the user know?" | Data transparency, consent alignment |
| 7 | "If I am a first-time user with low digital literacy, will I understand what the AI is doing?" | Plain language, layered explanations, no jargon |
| 8 | "Is there anything in this design that makes it easier to accept the AI's suggestion than to reject it?" | Asymmetric friction, visual hierarchy bias |

### 5.3 Usability Testing Governance Tasks

Add these tasks to usability test scripts when AI features are involved.

| # | Task | What to Observe |
|---|------|-----------------|
| 1 | "Find where the app tells you that AI is being used." | Can users locate the AI disclosure? |
| 2 | "The AI categorized this transaction as 'Entertainment.' Change it to 'Business Expense.'" | Is override discoverable and usable? |
| 3 | "Turn off AI-powered spending insights." | Can users find and use the opt-out? |
| 4 | "The AI denied your loan application. Find out why and appeal the decision." | Is the explanation understandable? Is the appeal path clear? |
| 5 | "The chatbot suggested a savings product. Decline the suggestion." | Is declining as easy as accepting? |
