Subsection 1 of 4

EU AI Act Risk Classification

Determine the risk tier before anything else. The tier drives everything downstream: governance intensity, required contracts, audit frequency.

Why it matters

EU AI Act Articles 5-6 and Annex III mandate risk classification. Getting this wrong means either over-engineering governance for low-risk systems or under-governing high-risk ones. The classification determines which of the 7 data contracts your agent must satisfy.

How we assess this

A structured YAML checklist walks through Annex III categories, intended purpose, autonomy level, and affected population. The output is a definitive tier (Minimal / Limited / High / Unacceptable) with evidence trail.

Subsection 2 of 4

Ethical Impact Assessment

Assess bias risk, societal impact, data ethics, and accountability before development. A 12-section structured template covers everything from fairness metrics to environmental impact.

Why it matters

Without structured ethics assessment, bias and harm are discovered in production — when they're expensive and damaging. The IAMA framework (Dutch Algorithm Register standard) requires documented impact assessment for all public-sector and high-risk AI.

How we assess this

12-section template covering purpose, stakeholders, bias risk, data ethics, transparency, accountability, societal impact, environmental impact, and human oversight. Scored 0-100 with per-section ratings. Signed off by 5 roles.

Subsection 3 of 4

Stakeholder Value Mapping

Map who benefits, who is affected, and who is at risk. Align stakeholder value with agent capabilities and cost profile.

Why it matters

AI systems impact multiple stakeholders differently. A lending agent serves credit risk teams but directly affects applicants' financial access. The value mapping ensures all parties are considered, not just the system's owner.

How we assess this

2x2 Impact-Influence quadrant chart plotting all stakeholders. Alignment table mapping each stakeholder to the value they receive, the risk they bear, and the oversight they need. Fed by C1 (agent scope), C4 (cost), C7 (decision impact).

Subsection 4 of 4

Acceptance Criteria & Metrics

Define what "good" looks like before building. Measurable acceptance criteria become the evaluation contract between Discovery and Development.

Why it matters

Without upfront criteria, teams debate quality subjectively. The acceptance criteria template ensures every AI initiative has measurable gates: accuracy thresholds, fairness bounds, latency limits, and cost ceilings — all defined before a single line of code.

How we assess this

Evaluation strategy template (YAML) defines metrics, thresholds, and frequencies. Kanban board tracks criteria status across the lifecycle. Gate readiness is computed from C2 health, C5 drift scores, and C3 eval traces.
